# Cognitive Load Theory & Mayer's Multimedia Learning Principles

This file answers questions about why learning content is structured the way it is — cognitive load, working memory limits, multimedia design principles, mengapa video pendek, why microlearning, screen design rationale, instructional video best practices, principles of multimedia learning.

## Quick answer

I design e-learning with **two evidence-based frameworks** that govern how the brain processes information:

1. **Cognitive Load Theory** (Sweller) — working memory has a finite capacity. Bad design overloads it; good design routes information through it efficiently.
2. **Mayer's 12 Principles of Multimedia Learning** — empirical rules for combining text, audio, and visuals so they reduce (not increase) load.

## Cognitive Load Theory in practice

There are three types of load. I optimize each:

* **Intrinsic load** (inherent difficulty of the topic) — I segment by chunking. A 30-minute compliance topic becomes 5 × 5-minute modules.
* **Extraneous load** (waste from bad design) — I aggressively cut: no decorative graphics, no on-screen text duplicating narration, no autoplay sound.
* **Germane load** (mental effort that builds schema) — I add: worked examples, retrieval-practice quizzes, branching scenarios.

## Mayer's principles I rely on most

| Principle | Practical rule | Where I apply it |
|---|---|---|
| **Coherence** | Cut anything not directly serving the objective | All Amartha video lectures — no decorative B-roll |
| **Signaling** | Highlight what matters (callouts, on-screen labels) | Modal Cycle Zero simulations |
| **Redundancy** | Don't show identical text + narration at the same time | Voiceover videos use captions, not full transcripts |
| **Spatial contiguity** | Put related text near related images | Storyboard reviews enforce this |
| **Modality** | Pair narration with graphics, not text + graphics | All instructional videos use voiceover + visuals |
| **Personalization** | Conversational tone, second-person ("you") | "Hai A-Team!" tone in lecture scripts |
| **Segmenting** | Let learners control pace; chunk into bites | Why Articulate Rise > Storyline for fast-rollout content |
| **Pre-training** | Teach key terms before complex procedures | TCP starts with vocabulary before scenario practice |

## Why short video duration matters

A common Q from recruiters: "kenapa video kamu durasi 5 menit bukan 30?" The honest answer:

* Working memory holds ~4 chunks for ~20 seconds. Even with rehearsal, attention drops sharply after **6 minutes** in instructional video (Guo et al., 2014, MOOC research).
* I aim for **3-6 minutes** per concept video, with retrieval-practice between segments.
* Modal Cycle Zero's high N-Gain (**44.63%**) on a large field-officer audience (**4,110**) is partially attributable to this segmentation discipline.

## Connection to other models

Cognitive Load Theory gives me the *why*. Mayer gives me the *how*. **Bloom's Taxonomy** (separate file) gives me the *what level to target*. Together: I pick the cognitive level → write objectives → design content that fits working memory → measure via N-Gain.
