# Kirkpatrick's 4 Levels of Training Evaluation

This file answers questions about training evaluation, measuring impact beyond completion rates, levels of evaluation, ROI of training, "did it actually work?", model evaluasi pelatihan, Kirkpatrick model.

## Quick reference

| Level | What it measures | How I capture it | Example from my work |
|---|---|---|---|
| **L1 — Reaction** | Did learners like it? | Post-course Likert-scale survey (1–4) | Aggregate **3.64 / 4** satisfaction across portfolio |
| **L2 — Learning** | Did they actually learn? | Pre-test + post-test → **N-Gain** (Hake's normalized gain) | Anti Harassment **N-Gain 64.58%**, Modal Cycle Zero **44.63%**, TCP **44.64%** |
| **L3 — Behavior** | Are they applying it on the job? | Manager/coach follow-up interviews, on-job observation, downstream KPIs | Tracked qualitatively for FO field performance after Modal Cycle Zero |
| **L4 — Results** | Did the business move? | Tie back to KPIs (loan disbursement, NPS, compliance incidents, retention) | Hardest level — relies on stakeholder collaboration; partial signal via post-Cycle-Zero product launch metrics |

## How I actually use it

* **L1 + L2 are non-negotiable.** Every course gets a satisfaction survey and pre/post + N-Gain. If a course can't be measured at L2, I push back on the design — usually means the objectives are mushy.
* **L3 is opportunistic.** I plan for it from the start (ask "what behavior would change?") but the actual measurement depends on stakeholder appetite.
* **L4 needs a sponsor.** I propose proxy metrics; the business owns the actual numbers.

## Why I prefer N-Gain over raw post-test

A 90 score post-test is meaningless without the pre-test. N-Gain corrects for ceiling effects — that's why Anti Harassment's **64.58%** N-Gain is impressive even though participants started at **85.14** baseline.
