# Other Instructional Design Frameworks I Use

This file answers questions about Gagné's 9 events, ARCS Model (Keller), Backward Design (Wiggins & McTighe), Merrill's First Principles, Universal Design for Learning (UDL), other ID frameworks beyond ADDIE/Bloom.

## Gagné's 9 Events of Instruction

A sequencing checklist for any single lesson. I use it to sanity-check that a video doesn't skip steps.

1. Gain attention (hook — opening scenario)
2. Inform learners of objectives ("by the end of this video you will…")
3. Stimulate recall of prior knowledge (callback to past module)
4. Present the content (core teaching)
5. Provide learning guidance (scaffolds, examples)
6. Elicit performance (practice — quiz, scenario)
7. Provide feedback (immediate, specific)
8. Assess performance (post-test, simulation)
9. Enhance retention & transfer (job aid, follow-up email)

In Modal Cycle Zero scripts I literally annotated each segment with the Gagné event it served — kept SMEs honest about why each scene exists.

## ARCS Model (Keller) — motivation by design

Used when learner motivation is the actual problem (compliance training, mandatory rollouts).

* **Attention** — open with relevance, not branding splash
* **Relevance** — explicitly tie to learner's job
* **Confidence** — sequence easy → hard so early wins build momentum
* **Satisfaction** — reward completion (certification, recognition, applied practice)

I lean heavily on ARCS for Anti-Harassment and Client Protection courses where compliance fatigue is real.

## Backward Design (Wiggins & McTighe)

Outcome-first design — start from "what should they be able to do?", then design assessments, then plan content. I claim this in my profile because it's how I run every project. ADDIE is the *process*; Backward Design is the *thinking* inside Analyze and Design.

Three stages:

1. **Identify desired results** (objectives, transfer goals)
2. **Determine acceptable evidence** (assessment blueprint)
3. **Plan learning experiences** (only after the first two are signed off)

This is why my courses produce honest N-Gain numbers — assessment is designed BEFORE content, not retrofitted after.

## Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

Five principles, useful when I'm designing simulation-heavy content:

1. **Problem-centered** — anchor learning in real tasks, not abstract concepts
2. **Activation** — surface prior experience first
3. **Demonstration** — show, don't just tell
4. **Application** — practice in context
5. **Integration** — connect to learner's job

The Modal Cycle Zero objection-handling simulations follow this pattern explicitly.

## Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

Three guiding principles for accessibility from the start (not retrofit):

* **Multiple means of engagement** — varied hooks, choice of paths
* **Multiple means of representation** — text + audio + visuals + captions
* **Multiple means of action and expression** — quizzes, scenarios, written reflection

For Indonesian audiences this also means **language accessibility** — Bahasa Indonesia primary, technical terms glossed, avoiding business jargon that exclude FO/BP audiences.

## When I pick which

* **Gagné** → script reviews (per-lesson sanity check)
* **ARCS** → mandatory / compliance content where motivation is the bottleneck
* **Backward Design** → every project, in ADDIE Analyze + Design
* **Merrill** → simulation-heavy or skill-acquisition modules
* **UDL** → from kickoff on every project, not bolted on
