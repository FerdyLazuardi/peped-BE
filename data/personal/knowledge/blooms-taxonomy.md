# How I use Bloom's Taxonomy

## Quick answer

I use the **revised Bloom's Taxonomy** (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) to write learning objectives at the right cognitive level, then design assessments that match that level. The six levels — Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create — give me a shared vocabulary with SMEs and a sanity check on whether a module is teaching at the right depth.

## How I actually apply it project by project

### Step 1 — Match objectives to the level the learner needs to operate at
Before I build anything, I write each learning objective with a Bloom verb that matches what the learner has to *do* on the job, not what's easiest to teach.

- **Modal Cycle Zero** (Field Officers training): the FOs don't just need to *Remember* the product features — they need to **Apply** them in client conversations and **Analyze** objections in real time. So objectives there look like *"Demonstrate how to handle a client objection about installment terms"* (Apply) rather than *"List the product features"* (Remember).
- **Training Client Protection**: the eight Client Protection principles must be **Applied** day-to-day, so objectives target Apply and Analyze levels — *"Identify which Client Protection principle is at risk in a given field scenario"* — not just memorization.
- **Dunia Geometri** (5th-grade math): kids need to **Understand** geometric concepts and then **Apply** them to solve problems. The interactive module is built around Apply-level practice rather than passive Remember-level definition recall.

### Step 2 — Align assessment items to the objective's level
A common mistake is teaching at Apply level but assessing at Remember level. I match them deliberately:

- **Apply-level objective** → scenario-based question or interactive simulation, not a multiple-choice definition. Modal Cycle Zero's simulations make the FO actually walk through a client conversation; the assessment isn't *"What is Cycle Zero?"* but *"In this scenario, what's the next best step?"*
- **Analyze-level objective** → compare/contrast or "spot the issue" question. Anti Harassment quizzes ask learners to classify situations as harassment or not, instead of just defining harassment.
- **Remember/Understand** → straight recall and explanation are fine when that's actually the job (e.g. compliance terms that must be quoted verbatim).

### Step 3 — Use it to validate scope
When SMEs over-stuff a module, Bloom's helps me push back constructively. I'll say *"Everything here is at Remember level — should this audience be operating at Apply by now?"* That re-anchors the conversation on outcomes instead of content volume.

### Step 4 — Use the Cognitive × Knowledge matrix when scope is unclear
The revised taxonomy crosses six cognitive levels with four knowledge types (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, Metacognitive). For complex curricula like Amartha System Architecture, I map each topic to a cell — e.g. *"Apply × Procedural"* for executing a workflow, *"Analyze × Conceptual"* for understanding why an architecture decision was made. This catches gaps where the module is heavy on Factual knowledge but light on the Procedural skill the learner actually needs.

## What I avoid

- **Verb-list cargo-culting.** Bloom's verbs aren't magic — *"Understand X"* is fine if the assessment really tests understanding. I use the verb lists as a starting point, not a checklist.
- **Climbing the pyramid for its own sake.** Not every module needs to push learners to Create. If the job is to *Apply*, stop at Apply.
- **Confusing affective/psychomotor work with cognitive work.** Bloom's cognitive domain isn't the only model — for soft-skill training (e.g. communication in Modal Cycle Zero) I lean on Krathwohl's affective domain or Dreyfus's skill-acquisition stages instead.

## Why it matters in my work

For client-facing teams (Field Officers, Agent Partners, A-Team), the cost of training at the wrong cognitive level is real — too shallow and learners can't perform on the job, too deep and you waste their time. Bloom's gives me a defensible answer to *"why is this module structured this way?"* when stakeholders push back.
